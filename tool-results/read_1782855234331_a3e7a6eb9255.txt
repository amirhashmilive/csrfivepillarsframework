  2506→### Methodology
  2507→
  2508→The methodology of the research was a mixed-methods design, combining qualitative field observation with quantitative survey research. The qualitative phase informed the development of the doctrinal architecture. The quantitative phase tested the architecture empirically.
  2509→
  2510→The qualitative phase consisted of extended field observation in implementation landscapes, structured interviews with institutional leaders and community stakeholders, document analysis of institutional reports and assessments, and case study development. The qualitative phase produced the doctrinal architecture — the Five Pillars, the Trust Layer, the diagnostic concepts — through inductive analysis of the patterns observed in the field. The qualitative phase is the source of the doctrine. The quantitative phase is the validation of the doctrine.
  2511→
  2512→The quantitative phase consisted of survey research, conducted with a sample of institutions and stakeholders selected to represent the diversity of institutional types and implementation contexts the framework claims to address. The quantitative phase tested whether the doctrinal architecture — operationalized as the CEI, the SAI, the WDC, the L, the Betterment Index, and the Trust Layer Maturity Score — corresponds to actual institutional performance and actual betterment outcomes. The quantitative phase is the empirical validation the chapter presents.

  2518→### Research Design
  2519→
  2520→The research design was a cross-sectional survey design with longitudinal follow-up. The cross-sectional component surveyed institutions and stakeholders at a single point in time, to establish the variation in Pillar Coherence, Trust Layer Maturity, and betterment outcomes across the sample. The longitudinal component followed a subset of institutions over multiple years, to establish whether changes in Pillar Coherence and Trust Layer Maturity produced changes in betterment outcomes over time.
  2521→
  2522→The research design was correlational, not experimental. The research did not randomly assign institutions to treatment and control conditions — institutions cannot be randomly assigned to architectural conditions. The research observed variation in architectural conditions across institutions and tested whether the variation correlated with variation in betterment outcomes. The correlational design is appropriate for the research question, which is whether the framework's architecture corresponds to institutional outcomes, not whether the architecture causes the outcomes.
  2523→
  2524→The correlational design has implications for causal inference. The research can establish correlation — institutions with higher Pillar Coherence tend to produce more demonstrable betterment. The research cannot establish causation through the design alone. Causal inference is supported by the longitudinal component (changes in architecture precede changes in outcome), by the theoretical architecture (the doctrinal logic of the Five Pillars provides the causal mechanism), and by the triangulation across methods (the qualitative phase provides the process evidence the quantitative phase cannot). The causal inference is reasonable but not experimental. The honest acknowledgment of this limit is part of the chapter's limitations section.
